    Student Learning and Development

    The role of a student affairs professional is to facilitate learning and growth in students. Creating opportunities that are challenging allow students to push themselves and develop new skills. As students navigate those challenges, it is imperative to listen carefully and identify resources and/or support structures. Practitioners must maintain high standards for themselves for continued learning and collaboration. As a student affairs professional, I approach my work with a focus on mutual accountability, advocacy for equity, and holistic development.

    Mutual Accountability

    Accountability is directly connected to high standards. Students and student affairs professionals alike commit to institutions of higher education with aspirations of learning and growth. I believe accountability hinges on the concepts Sanford’s Theory of Challenge and Support. Accountability is inherently a supportive measure to help individuals, but there is challenge presented in achieving the high standards. By setting clear expectations, managing deadlines, communicating effectively, and taking ownership of both successes and mistakes, I display the level of integrity I expect for myself. I make these personal expectations clear so that students and colleagues can join me in a process of mutual accountability.

    Advocacy for Equity

    Student affairs professionals have the responsibility to build inclusive and supportive communities for all students. To do this, I try to recognize the unique needs and circumstances of each student. I check my biases and try to stay just as committed to unlearning as I am to learning. Practitioners must recognize opportunities to use a seat at the table to advocate for equity. With accountability in mind, student affairs professionals have the duty to look critically at their own department or institution and have the courage to speak up when systems or practices are not equitable.

    Advocacy for Equity

    Students are more than their degree program or student organization. They are mosaics of culture, identity, dreams, fears, and experiences we may never begin to understand during a short time together. I recognize that it is important for a student to develop in all areas of their life, not just those areas that align with university activities. It is the responsibility of a student affairs professional to ask questions that help unpack motivations and influences so that we understand the scope of the support needed. I focus on holistic development by taking what they have learned in class or in their interactions with university staff to their personal interests or professional aspirations.

    For the holistic development of students, which is the development of the student in many or all areas of their life, practitioners must be able to apply a range of theories to inform their work. By identifying the unique needs of students related to their individual experiences and identities, practitioners can provide appropriate levels of challenge and support. The following subsections offer evidence of the skills and competencies I have gained related to SLD outcomes, as outlined in ACPA/NASPA’s Professional Competency Area for Student Affairs Educators.

     

    Understanding Theory

    I have gained knowledge about various student learning and development theories that influence my daily work. I have found Nevitt Sanford’s Theory of Challenge and Support to be particularly influential in my development of action plans in student conduct. To provide or recommend the best support, I have to understand how the student's identities and circumstances impact their experience. One example of this understanding is a group academic poster presentation focused on Undocumented Students in Higher Education. When creating action plans, I try to offer students opportunities that will challenge them to think or behave differently while incorporating appropriate support structures that will help them make those changes.  In an academic research project, my group focused on Assessing the Intellectual Well-Being of First Generation College Students at Indiana University Bloomington During COVID-19.  Since completing this assessment, I have been intentional about having manageable expectations for the action plan items for first generation students, as we saw declines in their intellectual well-being since the pandemic. 

     

    Additionally, I have learned how to identify gaps in literature and work with existing theories or models to apply concepts. Specifically, I wrote a paper about the ways that white sorority members can utilize Janet Helms’ White Racial Identity Development (WRID) framework to be better allies to Black sorority women. I applied these concepts while teaching the Rho Gamma Facilitator Training (EDUC-U208) course, described below. As a sorority woman, I applied my understanding of WRID to the programming my international organization is offering by writing an op-ed for the Association of Fraternal Advisors Essentials publication titled Developing Anti-Racist Members Through Organizational Change.

    Teaching

    As a co-instructor for a section of the Foundations of Residential Leadership course (EDUC-U450), I taught students about student development theories, discussed the influence of identity, and shared personal examples of development. The smaller size of this class allowed me to do frequent formative assessments, regularly solicit feedback about instruction techniques, and make adjustments accordingly. Additionally, as a co-instructor for Rho Gamma Facilitator Training (EDUC-U208), I facilitated conversations on race, ethnicity, gender, and class. These conversations equipped the Rho Gammas to be effective recruitment counselors and better understand how to support potential new members with different identities.     Foundational Outcomes

    • Articulate theories and models that describe the development of college students and the conditions and practices that facilitate holistic development (e.g. learning, psychosocial and identity development, cognitive-structural, typological, environmental, and moral).

    • Articulate how race, ethnicity, nationality, class, gender, age, sexual orientation, gender identity, dis/ability, and religious belief can influence development during the college years.

    • Articulate one’s own developmental journey in relation to formal theories.

    • Construct learning outcomes for both daily practice as well as teaching and training activities.

    • Assess teaching, learning, and training and incorporate the results into practice.

     

    Intermediate Outcomes

    • Utilize theory-to-practice models to inform individual or unit practice.

    • Identify and take advantage of opportunities
      for curriculum and program development to encourage continual learning and developmental growth.

    • Construct effective programs, lesson plans, and syllabi.

    • Teach, train, and practice in such a way that utilizes the assessment of learning outcomes to inform future practice.
