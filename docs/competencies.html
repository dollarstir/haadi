ACPA / NASPA

Professional competencies

How Residence Life experiences connect to foundational student affairs competence.

Some of the most meaningful parts of a student’s college experience happen outside of the classroom—in residence halls, during late-night conversations, roommate disagreements, shared meals, and learning to live in community. The ACPA/NASPA Professional Competencies describe core knowledge, skills, and values for our field. Below I highlight four areas especially central to my path: Personal and Ethical Foundations; Advising and Supporting; Social Justice and Inclusion; and Organizational and Human Resources.

Personal and Ethical Foundations (PEF)

Ethical awareness is essential when staff work closely with students in sensitive situations. I practice listening to every perspective in roommate or conduct conversations before responding, documenting carefully, protecting confidentiality, and following policy. I seek supervisor guidance when situations are complex and work to maintain boundaries and wellness so I can remain present for students with consistency.

Advising and Supporting (A/S)

Meaningful interactions often happen informally—hall conversations about stress, roommates, or identity. I focus on active listening and questions that build students’ confidence in their own decision-making while still connecting them to mental health, academic, and financial resources when needed. I aim for empathy and cultural humility across differences in background and experience.

Social Justice and Inclusion (SJI)

Residence halls gather students from many backgrounds daily. Inclusion means encouraging dialogue, addressing harm, and supporting programs that elevate diverse perspectives. I reflect on how policies and norms land differently for marginalized groups—including international students—and on my own position within systems of power, so I can advocate for equitable belonging.

Organizational and Human Resources (OHR)

Behind student interactions sit coordination with custodial, maintenance, dining, and assignments teams; supervising RAs; and crisis response that follows protocol while staying attentive to student needs. I learn daily from collaboration among graduate staff, professionals, and student leaders, and I continue to build skill in planning, supervision, and responsible use of resources.

I also grow in Assessment, Evaluation, and Research—using observation, feedback, and documentation to improve programs. See Assessment for a focused narrative on evidence-informed practice.
